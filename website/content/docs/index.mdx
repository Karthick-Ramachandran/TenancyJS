**TenancyJS** gives every framework and ORM the same tenant-context and isolation contract — without
replacing the framework or ORM you already use. Tenant identity follows the async execution scope, so
your queries stay scoped without threading a `tenantId` through every call.

And the guarantee that matters:

> Any tenant-aware data access without a valid tenant context **throws** — it never returns another
> tenant's data.

## Why it exists

The dangerous bug in multi-tenancy is the one that _doesn't_ throw. Forget a `WHERE tenant_id = ?` and
nothing breaks in the demo — you just quietly returned one customer's data to another. TenancyJS makes
the safe path the default: no valid tenant context, no data.

## What you get

- **Three isolation strategies behind one contract** — [row-level](/docs/row-level),
  [schema-per-tenant](/docs/schema-per-tenant), and [database-per-tenant](/docs/database-per-tenant).
- **Adapters that enforce isolation inside your ORM** — Prisma, Knex, Lucid, TypeORM, Sequelize, Mongoose.
- **Framework integrations** that bind tenant context to the request lifecycle — Express, Next.js,
  AdonisJS, NestJS.
- **An operational [CLI](/docs/cli)** to inspect, create, migrate, and run against your live tenants.

## Honesty by design

Every "supported" cell in the [capability matrix](/docs/capability-matrix) is backed by a two-tenant
adversarial isolation test on a real database. Nothing is marked supported on faith — and the CLI's
`tenant check` warns you about any untested combination instead of pretending it's production-ready.

<Callout type="info">
  Tenant identity is **not** authorization. Your app still owns auth. TenancyJS owns exactly one thing:
  that one tenant's data never becomes another's.
</Callout>
